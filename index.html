1) Introduction — “What is ISAGO & why does it exist?”

Problem → consequences → why a global audit + sharing model matters.

Ground operations reality Many handlers, many airports, many airline requirements → variation + risk. Operational context
→
Risks & outcomes Ground hazards affecting flight safety, aircraft damage, personnel injuries, security threats, delays & costs. Why it matters
→
Oversight gap Minimal direct oversight over GSP selection/licensing, training, auditing, reporting.
Regulators often hold airlines responsible for GSP oversight
Why airlines struggle
→
Current situation (before ISAGO) Redundant audits: multiple airlines audit the same GSP, in different ways → inconsistent results. Inefficient & noisy
→
ISAGO idea One standardized audit system + recognized standards + trained auditors + audit sharing. Single baseline
Reader takeaway
  • ISAGO exists to reduce operational risk and reduce redundant audits through a shared, standard system.

2) ISAGO Model — “Two-level audits → Registration + Station Accreditation”

This is the heart of ISAGO: Headquarters/Corporate + Station audit(s).

GHSP decides to pursue ISAGO Defines scope of services + stations to be covered. Applicant
Audit scope (disciplines) Always ORM + operational disciplines (typical): LOD, PAB, HDL, AGM, CGM.
ORM + {LOD,PAB,HDL,AGM,CGM}
→
Headquarters / Corporate Audit (MHQ) Checks corporate policies, processes & procedures (network-wide control).
Usually first step for initial registration
Audit type: MHQ
Station Audit (STN) Checks local implementation at a specific station (how work is really done on the ground). Audit type: STN
→
Registration + Station Accreditation After: (1) MHQ + (2) at least one STN closed and approved → GHSP appears on ISAGO Registry; station(s) accredited. Outcome
Important “modern program” details (keeps you accurate today)
  • Combined Audit (CTN) may be used in specific cases (HQ near station; limited station network).
  • Initial station accreditation audit must happen within a defined window after the initial HQ audit (program-controlled timelines).
  • Registration/Accreditation validity is on a fixed cycle (renewal required before expiry).

3) ISAGO Documentation System — “Standards → Checklists → Reports”

What auditors use, what GHSPs map to, and what gets produced.

Standards layer GOSARPs (requirements) organized inside the ISAGO standards structure (ORM + operational disciplines). “What good looks like”
→
Program rules layer Program Manual defines audit types, timelines, closure rules, approvals, renewals. “How ISAGO is run”
→
Audit execution layer Audit software/checklists + documentation assessment + implementation assessment → findings recorded. “How audits happen”
→
Outputs Audit Report + Findings + closure evidence → IATA quality review/approval → Registry listing & report sharing protocol. “What everyone consumes”
How the audit starts (DA → IA) in practical terms
  • DA (Documentation Assessment): auditor validates the GHSP’s gap analysis + controlled documentation before onsite implementation checks.
  • IA (Implementation Assessment): onsite/operational verification: interviews, observation, sampling, records, compliance evidence.

4) IOSA & ISAGO — “How they relate (and how they don’t)”

IOSA = airline operations; ISAGO = ground operations providers & ground functions.

IOSA Airline operational safety audit program (airline-centric scope). Airline ops
↔
ISAGO Ground operations audit program (GHSP/airline-as-handler) with detailed ground disciplines. Ground ops
→
Key idea ISAGO goes deeper operationally on ground functions than IOSA’s ground sections because it’s purpose-built for ground operations. Depth on ground

5) ISAGO Benefits — “Who benefits and how”

Same program, different value per stakeholder.

For Airlines Fewer redundant audits + standardized visibility into handler safety + access to shared reports (protocol-based). Efficiency + oversight
+
For GHSPs One benchmark system; clearer expectations; reduced audit chaos; improved safety management + credibility. Benchmark + trust
+
For Regulators / Airports Uniform standards across handlers; can complement oversight; could support airport concession/approval decisions. Uniformity

→
Pool Airlines Commit to perform an allocated number of station audits each year.
Also: unconditional access to shared audit reports
Contribute + consume
→

→

→
Audit Reports shared securely IATA provides controlled access to reports and keeps them in a secure environment (per agreed protocol). Trust & reuse

9) ISAGO Costs — “Who pays for what” (as described in the deck)

Costs are split by audit type and who performs it.

Corporate / HQ audit cost Conducted by independent audit organizations (AOs/GOAs); GHSP covers HQ audit cost + travel/accom (deck example shows a 2-year cycle). Paid by GHSP
+
Station audit cost Station audits conducted by auditing airlines in the pool; auditing airlines cover their own travel/accom costs inherent to station audits (deck model). Paid by auditing airline
→
Net effect Creates an incentive structure: GHSP invests in corporate control; airlines invest in oversight at stations; everyone reuses shared reports. Shared efficiency

11) IGOM — “Standardizing airline ground manuals”

IGOM is a separate but related standardization push: reduce variations across airline GOMs.

Today (problem) Each airline publishes its own Ground Operations Manual (GOM) → for same aircraft type, variations can be big. Variation
→
Impact Variations → increased safety risk, aircraft damage risk, inefficiency for handlers. Operational friction
→
IGOM concept Standardized IATA Ground Operations Manual that airlines can adopt as a “core” requirement set. Common baseline
